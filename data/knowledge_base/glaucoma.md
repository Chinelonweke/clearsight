# Glaucoma

## Overview
Glaucoma is a group of eye conditions that damage the optic nerve,
usually caused by abnormally high intraocular pressure (IOP). It is the
leading cause of irreversible blindness in Nigeria and across sub-Saharan Africa.
It is often called the "silent thief of sight" because most forms progress
without pain or noticeable symptoms until significant damage has occurred.

## Types
- Open-angle glaucoma (most common, gradual, painless)
- Angle-closure glaucoma (acute, emergency — rapid onset)
- Normal-tension glaucoma (optic nerve damage despite normal IOP)
- Secondary glaucoma (caused by injury, surgery, or another eye condition)
- Congenital glaucoma (present from birth)

## Symptoms to watch for
- Gradual loss of peripheral (side) vision — often unnoticed until advanced
- Tunnel vision in late stages
- Blurred or hazy vision
- Acute angle-closure: sudden severe eye pain, headache, nausea, vomiting,
  blurred vision, halos around lights, redness — THIS IS AN EMERGENCY
- In congenital: enlarged eyes, excessive tearing, light sensitivity in infants

## Triage guidance
- Acute angle-closure attack → EMERGENCY, immediate same-day referral, do not delay
- Sudden vision loss with eye pain and nausea → EMERGENCY
- Suspected chronic open-angle with vision changes → URGENT, within 3 days
- Known glaucoma patient with new symptoms → URGENT, within one week
- Routine IOP monitoring for known stable glaucoma → ROUTINE, schedule normally
- Family history with no current symptoms → ROUTINE, book screening

## Risk factors
- Family history of glaucoma (3–9× increased risk)
- Age over 40 (African populations develop glaucoma earlier and more severely)
- High intraocular pressure (>21 mmHg)
- Thin central cornea
- Diabetes mellitus and hypertension
- Previous eye injury or surgery
- Long-term use of corticosteroid eye drops or systemic steroids
- High myopia (short-sightedness) for open-angle; hyperopia for angle-closure

## What the AI should ask
- Do you have a family history of glaucoma or blindness?
- Have you noticed any loss of side vision or tunnel vision?
- Are you experiencing eye pain, headache, or nausea?
- Do you see halos or rainbows around lights, especially at night?
- Have you been on steroid eye drops for a long time?
- Have you had a previous eye injury or surgery?
- How long have you had these symptoms?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Cataracts (gradual vision loss but lens-related, not optic nerve)
- Diabetic retinopathy (also causes progressive vision loss)
- Age-related macular degeneration (central vision loss)
- Uveitis (can cause secondary pressure elevation)
- Migraine with visual aura (transient halos, resolves quickly)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- African-descent patients have higher prevalence, earlier onset, and faster
  progression of primary open-angle glaucoma than other populations.
- Many patients present late due to low awareness and limited access to care.
- Sickle cell trait can be associated with glaucoma — screen appropriately.
- Consider asking about traditional eye medicine use, which can worsen IOP.
