# Conjunctivitis (Apollo / Pink Eye)

## Overview
Conjunctivitis is inflammation of the conjunctiva — the thin, clear tissue
covering the white part of the eye and the inner surface of the eyelids.
It is the most common eye condition seen in Nigerian clinics and is colloquially
known as "Apollo" (derived from the Apollo 11 epidemic outbreak in 1969).
Most cases are infectious, and bacterial and viral forms spread rapidly in
schools, households, and crowded environments. Most cases resolve without
permanent damage, but severe or untreated cases can cause complications.

## Types
- Viral conjunctivitis: Most common; watery discharge; highly contagious;
  often associated with upper respiratory tract infection or adenovirus.
- Bacterial conjunctivitis: Purulent (pus-like) discharge; can be caused by
  Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, Haemophilus, or Neisseria gonorrhoeae (serious).
- Allergic conjunctivitis: Itching is the dominant symptom; associated with
  dust, pollen, animals, or contact lens solution. Not contagious.
- Neonatal conjunctivitis (ophthalmia neonatorum): In newborns, can be caused
  by gonorrhoea or chlamydia — potentially sight-threatening → URGENT.
- Chemical/toxic conjunctivitis: From eye drops, chlorinated water, or irritants.

## Symptoms to watch for
- Red, pink, or bloodshot eyes
- Watery or mucous/pus discharge (discharge character indicates type)
- Itching (dominant in allergic type)
- Gritty or foreign body sensation
- Crusting of eyelids especially in the morning
- Swelling of conjunctiva (chemosis)
- Mild photophobia (sensitivity to light)
- One eye affected initially, spreading to second eye within 24–48 hours (viral/bacterial)
- Severe pain or vision loss → suggests more serious condition, NOT simple conjunctivitis

## Triage guidance
- Severe eye pain with vision loss → NOT conjunctivitis — EMERGENCY, reassess
- Newborn with eye discharge (any age under 28 days) → URGENT, same day
- Suspected gonococcal conjunctivitis (profuse purulent discharge, sexually active) → URGENT
- Bacterial conjunctivitis not responding after 3 days of drops → URGENT
- Simple viral or bacterial conjunctivitis with discharge but no pain/vision loss → ROUTINE
- Allergic conjunctivitis, chronic or recurrent → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Close contact with infected person (viral/bacterial spreads rapidly)
- Touching eyes with unwashed hands
- Sharing towels, pillows, or eye makeup
- Contact lens use (increases infection risk)
- Exposure to allergens (dust, pollen, smoke, animals)
- Swimming in chlorinated or untreated pools
- Newborns born to mothers with STIs (gonorrhoea, chlamydia)
- Living in crowded or low-hygiene environments

## What the AI should ask
- Which eye is affected — or both?
- What does the discharge look like — watery, mucousy, or yellow/green pus?
- Is it very itchy? (allergic) or more gritty/burning? (infectious)
- Do you have any pain or vision changes? (rules out more serious conditions)
- Do you have a cold, runny nose, or sore throat? (suggests viral)
- Has anyone around you (family, school) had similar symptoms recently?
- Are you wearing contact lenses?
- Is the patient a newborn? How old?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Uveitis (eye pain, photophobia, blurred vision — more serious)
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma (pain, halos, nausea — emergency)
- Corneal ulcer (pain, photophobia, visible opacity)
- Subconjunctival haemorrhage (blood-red appearance, no discharge — usually benign)
- Dry eye syndrome (burning, no discharge pattern of infection)
- Episcleritis (sectoral redness, mild discomfort)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- "Apollo" outbreaks occur frequently in Nigeria, especially in school settings
  and during harmattan season when dust levels are high.
- Viral conjunctivitis has no antibiotic treatment — educate patients to avoid
  unnecessary antibiotic eye drops, which are overused in Nigerian markets.
- Neonatal conjunctivitis (ophthalmia neonatorum) must be treated urgently to
  prevent corneal scarring and blindness.
- Advise strict hygiene measures: handwashing, no sharing of towels, staying
  away from school or work for 2–5 days in active infection.
- Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (VKC) is a severe allergic form particularly
  prevalent in young Nigerian males — ask about intense itching and mucous strings.
