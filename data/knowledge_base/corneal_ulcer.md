# Corneal Ulcer (Corneal Abscess / Keratitis)

## Overview
A corneal ulcer is an open sore or erosion on the cornea — the clear,
dome-shaped front surface of the eye. It is caused by infection (bacterial,
fungal, viral, or amoebic), injury, or severe dry eye. Corneal ulcers are
ophthalmic emergencies because the cornea can be rapidly and permanently scarred
or perforated if treatment is delayed, leading to blindness. Fungal corneal
ulcers are particularly prevalent in tropical agricultural settings like Nigeria,
where eye injuries from plant material are common.

## Types / Causes
- Bacterial keratitis: Pseudomonas (especially in contact lens wearers),
  Staphylococcus, Streptococcus. Rapid progression.
- Fungal keratitis (keratomycosis): Fusarium, Aspergillus. Common in Nigeria
  from trauma with vegetative matter (grass, sticks, crops). Slower onset
  but harder to treat. VERY common in agricultural workers.
- Viral keratitis: Herpes simplex virus (HSV) — recurrent, causes characteristic
  branching (dendritic) ulcer pattern.
- Acanthamoeba keratitis: Rare but severe — associated with contact lens wearers
  who use tap water or swim with lenses in.
- Neurotrophic keratitis: Loss of corneal sensation causing epithelial breakdown.

## Symptoms to watch for
- Severe eye pain (often the most prominent complaint)
- Marked photophobia (difficulty tolerating light)
- Excessive tearing and discharge
- Red eye (especially around the limbus/corneal edge)
- Blurred or decreased vision
- White or grey spot or opacity visible on the cornea (may be visible to patient)
- Sensation of foreign body in the eye
- Swollen, stuck-together eyelids
- In advanced cases: corneal perforation (sudden loss of pain + aqueous leakage)
  → EMERGENCY

## Triage guidance
- Any suspected corneal ulcer with pain + opacity + photophobia → URGENT to EMERGENCY
  — do NOT send patient away; needs same-day or immediate assessment
- Contact lens wearer with painful red eye + white spot → EMERGENCY, remove lens now
- Farmer or agricultural worker with eye injury from plant material + pain → URGENT,
  high suspicion for fungal keratitis
- Corneal perforation (sudden relief of pain, visible hole, soft eye) → EMERGENCY
- Recurrent HSV keratitis flare on treatment → URGENT, review within 24–48 hours
- Previous corneal scar, no current symptoms → ROUTINE monitoring

## Risk factors
- Contact lens wear (especially overnight or with tap water — major risk)
- Eye trauma, especially with plant material, soil, or dust (agriculture workers)
- Occupation: farming, construction, outdoor labour
- Dry eye (disrupts corneal surface protection)
- Previous herpes simplex eye infection (recurrence)
- Immunosuppression (HIV, diabetes, malnutrition)
- Use of topical corticosteroids without antifungal/antiviral cover
- Poorly fitted or shared traditional eye medications / drops

## What the AI should ask
- Do you have severe pain in the eye — even when it is closed?
- Is your eye very sensitive to light?
- Can you see a white or grey spot on the front of your eye?
- Have you had any injury to the eye recently — from a twig, grass, dust, or something hitting it?
- Do you wear contact lenses? Do you sleep in them or rinse them with tap water?
- Do you have diabetes or HIV?
- Have you had herpes (cold sores on your lips) or a previous similar eye problem?
- Have you put any traditional drops or substances into your eye?
- How long have you had these symptoms?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Conjunctivitis (no severe pain, no opacity, vision preserved)
- Anterior uveitis / iritis (pain and photophobia but cornea usually clear)
- Corneal abrasion (pain + epithelial defect without infiltrate — from scratch or FB)
- Subconjunctival haemorrhage (red but painless, no opacity)
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma (pain + blurred vision + halos + hard eye)
- Episcleritis / scleritis (redness, pain, but different localisation)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Fungal corneal ulcer is the most important and common severe corneal infection
  in Nigeria. It predominantly affects farmers and outdoor workers who sustain
  plant-matter eye injuries.
- There is often a delay in diagnosis because fungal ulcers initially look like
  bacterial ulcers and only respond to antifungal agents (natamycin, voriconazole).
- Traditional eye medicines (including herbal drops, urine, breast milk, or saliva)
  are commonly applied before hospital attendance in Nigeria and significantly
  worsen corneal ulcers — always ask about this with sensitivity.
- The harmattan dust and dry season creates conditions for increased corneal
  surface damage and subsequent infection.
- Corneal blindness from scarring after healed ulcer is significant — refer all
  corneal ulcer patients to prevent this outcome.
- Topical steroids are absolutely contraindicated as first-line treatment
  for any undiagnosed corneal infection.
