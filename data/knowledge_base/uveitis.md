# Uveitis

## Overview
Uveitis is inflammation of the uvea — the middle layer of the eye comprising
the iris, ciliary body, and choroid. It is a potentially sight-threatening
condition that can occur at any age, including in children. It accounts for
approximately 10–15% of blindness in developing countries and is an important
cause of preventable vision loss in Nigeria. It requires prompt diagnosis and
treatment to prevent complications including cataracts, glaucoma, and permanent
retinal damage.

## Types (by anatomical location)
- Anterior uveitis (iritis): Inflammation of the iris and ciliary body —
  most common type. Presents with acute eye pain, redness, photophobia.
- Intermediate uveitis: Inflammation of the vitreous and peripheral retina.
  Floaters and mild blurring predominate; less pain.
- Posterior uveitis (choroiditis/retinitis): Inflammation of the choroid or
  retina. Vision blurring, floaters, can progress silently.
- Panuveitis: All layers involved — often the most severe form.

## Aetiology (causes)
- Idiopathic (most common — no identifiable cause found)
- Autoimmune / systemic: Ankylosing spondylitis, sarcoidosis, juvenile
  idiopathic arthritis, inflammatory bowel disease, Behçet's disease
- Infectious: Toxoplasmosis (very common in Nigeria — from undercooked meat
  and contaminated water), tuberculosis, HIV/AIDS, herpes viruses,
  cytomegalovirus, syphilis
- HLA-B27 associated (ankylosing spondylitis, reactive arthritis)
- Trauma (sympathetic ophthalmia after penetrating injury)
- Masquerade syndromes (lymphoma mimicking uveitis)

## Symptoms to watch for
- Eye pain (can be severe in anterior uveitis, absent in posterior)
- Redness (ciliary injection — redness around the iris, not just the whites)
- Photophobia (sensitivity to light — often prominent)
- Blurred vision
- Floaters (especially in intermediate and posterior uveitis)
- Small, irregular, or poorly reactive pupil (posterior synechiae)
- Decreased vision in severe or neglected cases

## Triage guidance
- Acute painful red eye with photophobia and blurred vision → URGENT, within 24–48 hours
- Posterior uveitis with significant vision loss → URGENT, same day or next day
- Uveitis in a child → URGENT (juvenile idiopathic arthritis-related uveitis
  can be painless and cause silent damage — "white uveitis")
- Uveitis in HIV-positive patient → URGENT (wide differential, immunocompromised)
- Recurrent anterior uveitis, currently not in flare, requesting review → ROUTINE
- First episode now resolving on treatment → follow up within 1 week

## Risk factors
- Previous episodes of uveitis (recurrence is common)
- Known autoimmune or rheumatic disease (ankylosing spondylitis, IBD, sarcoidosis)
- HIV/AIDS or other immunocompromised states
- Exposure to TB (very relevant in Nigeria)
- History of toxoplasmosis or other parasitic infections
- Prior eye injury or surgery
- Low socioeconomic status / malnutrition (increases infectious causes)

## What the AI should ask
- Is your eye red, painful, and sensitive to light?
- Is your vision blurred?
- Do you see floaters?
- Is this the first time, or have you had similar episodes before?
- Do you have any joint pain, back pain, or skin rashes?
- Have you been diagnosed with any autoimmune condition?
- Do you have HIV or are you on any immune-suppressing medication?
- Have you ever been treated for tuberculosis?
- Have you eaten undercooked or raw meat recently?
- Was there any injury to your eye?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma (pain + red eye, but with halos and nausea — check IOP)
- Conjunctivitis (red eye but no significant pain, photophobia, or vision loss)
- Scleritis (deep, boring, severe pain — rare but serious)
- Corneal ulcer (pain + opacity on cornea — visible on exam)
- Endophthalmitis (post-surgical or post-traumatic — very severe and urgent)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Toxoplasmosis is a major cause of posterior uveitis in Nigeria and should be
  considered in any patient with posterior uveitis, especially retinochoroiditis.
- Tuberculosis is endemic in Nigeria and is an important infectious cause of uveitis
  — always ask about TB history, contact, or symptoms (cough, weight loss, sweats).
- HIV-related uveitis (including CMV retinitis in late-stage HIV) is increasingly
  seen — always check HIV status in unexplained or recurrent uveitis.
- Behçet's disease, though rare globally, has higher prevalence along the
  ancient Silk Road and in some West African populations — ask about oral ulcers
  and genital sores.
- Sarcoidosis can be underdiagnosed in Nigerians — consider in bilateral or
  recurrent uveitis with systemic symptoms.
