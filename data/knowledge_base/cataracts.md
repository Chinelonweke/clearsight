# Cataracts

## Overview
A cataract is a clouding of the eye's natural lens, which lies behind the iris
and pupil. It is the leading cause of blindness worldwide and the most common
cause of treatable blindness in Nigeria. Cataracts develop slowly and are
largely age-related, but can occur at any age due to injury, disease, or
congenital factors. Surgery is the only effective treatment and is highly
successful when performed in time.

## Types
- Age-related (senile) cataract — most common; nuclear, cortical, or posterior subcapsular
- Congenital cataract — present at birth or develops in early childhood
- Traumatic cataract — caused by eye injury (blunt or penetrating)
- Secondary cataract — caused by other conditions (diabetes, uveitis, steroids)
- Radiation cataract — rare, from UV or ionizing radiation exposure

## Symptoms to watch for
- Gradual blurring or dimming of vision (like looking through frosted glass)
- Increased sensitivity to glare and bright lights (especially night driving)
- Halos around lights
- Colours appear faded, yellowed, or brownish
- Frequent changes in eyeglass or contact lens prescription
- Double vision in one eye
- Difficulty reading in low light
- In advanced cases: near-total or total loss of vision ("white eye" in late stage)

## Triage guidance
- Sudden vision loss or white reflex in a child → URGENT, within 2 days (amblyopia risk)
- Traumatic cataract with eye injury → URGENT, assess same day
- Advanced cataract with very poor vision affecting daily function → URGENT, within a week
- Gradual blurring over months without functional impairment → ROUTINE
- Routine pre-surgical assessment for known cataracts → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Age (most common after 60)
- Diabetes mellitus (accelerates lens clouding significantly)
- Prolonged exposure to sunlight / UV radiation (very relevant in Nigeria)
- Smoking and excessive alcohol use
- Prolonged corticosteroid use (topical or systemic)
- Previous eye surgery, inflammation, or trauma
- Family history of early cataracts
- Malnutrition (vitamin deficiencies, especially Vitamin C and E)

## What the AI should ask
- How long have you noticed the blurring?
- Is the blurring in one eye or both?
- Do you have difficulty seeing in bright light or at night?
- Do you see halos around lights?
- Have you had a recent eye injury?
- Do you have diabetes?
- Have you been using any eye drops for a long time?
- Has your glasses prescription been changing frequently?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Glaucoma (also causes gradual vision loss — check for IOP and visual field)
- Diabetic retinopathy (retinal cause, not lens)
- Age-related macular degeneration (central vision affected)
- Refractive error (correctable with glasses — does vision improve with pinhole?)
- Corneal opacity or scar (front of eye, visible on inspection)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- High UV exposure in Nigeria accelerates age-related cataract development.
- Many patients present late with "mature" or "hypermature" cataracts that are
  harder to operate and carry more surgical risk.
- Encourage early referral before the cataract becomes white and hard.
- Cataract surgery (phacoemulsification or ECCE) is curative — emphasise this
  to dispel fear of surgery in patient communication.
- Ask about use of traditional medicine or eye drops in the affected eye.
