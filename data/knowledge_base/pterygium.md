# Pterygium

## Overview
A pterygium (plural: pterygia) is a benign, fleshy, wedge-shaped growth of
conjunctival tissue that extends onto the cornea, typically from the nasal side.
It is extremely common in tropical and subtropical regions like Nigeria due to
high UV exposure, dust, wind, and dry climates. The name comes from the Greek
word for "wing" due to its wing-like appearance. While usually benign and
slow-growing, a pterygium can impair vision if it grows across the central
cornea, and it commonly causes cosmetic concern and surface discomfort.

## Characteristics
- Typically grows from the nasal corner of the eye toward the cornea
- Can occur on both sides (bilateral) or in both eyes
- Usually slow-growing over years to decades
- Has a head (corneal part), neck, and body (conjunctival part)
- Distinguished from pinguecula (a similar yellow bump that does NOT grow onto
  the cornea — pinguecula is precursor and less serious)

## Symptoms to watch for
- Visible fleshy growth on the white of the eye (usually nasal side)
- Cosmetic concern — "something growing on my eye"
- Redness and inflammation of the growth, especially in dusty/dry conditions
- Foreign body sensation or grittiness
- Dryness and irritation
- Blurred or distorted vision if the growth approaches or crosses the visual axis
- Induced astigmatism (irregular corneal curvature from pterygium traction)
- Restricted eye movement in very large or recurrent pterygia (rare)
- NOT typically painful — significant pain suggests another diagnosis

## Triage guidance
- Vision-threatening pterygium approaching or covering the pupil → URGENT,
  surgical referral within 2 weeks
- Rapidly growing pterygium over the past few months → URGENT, assess promptly
- Severe inflammation of the pterygium with significant redness → URGENT,
  differentiate from episcleritis or scleritis
- Cosmetic concern + irritation, not approaching visual axis → ROUTINE
- Pinguecula with irritation, no corneal involvement → ROUTINE
- Routine pre-surgical assessment for stable pterygium → ROUTINE
- Post-surgical review after pterygium excision → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- UV radiation exposure — primary and most important cause
- Living or working outdoors in a tropical/sunny climate (extremely relevant in Nigeria)
- Dust and wind exposure (harmattan, farming, construction)
- Dry environments (low humidity areas in northern Nigeria)
- Male sex (men more commonly affected — likely due to more outdoor work)
- Age 20–50 (peak incidence)
- Light-coloured irides (less melanin protection — less relevant in Nigerians
  but UV still causative regardless of iris colour)
- Inadequate or no use of UV-protective eyewear

## What the AI should ask
- Have you noticed a fleshy or pink growth on the white part of your eye?
- Is it on one eye or both?
- How long have you had it? Is it growing quickly?
- Is your vision affected — blurry or distorted?
- Does it cause redness, irritation, or a gritty feeling?
- Do you work outdoors or have significant sun/dust exposure?
- Have you had pterygium surgery before? (recurrence is common)
- Is this mainly a cosmetic concern or is it affecting your vision?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Pinguecula (yellow elevation on conjunctiva, does NOT grow onto cornea)
- Conjunctival squamous cell carcinoma (rare, irregular surface, rapid growth —
  any atypical appearing lesion needs biopsy)
- Conjunctival naevus (pigmented lesion, stable)
- Limbal dermoid (smooth, opaque, present from birth)
- Episcleritis / scleritis (redness and inflammation without growth)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Pterygium is one of the most common conditions seen in Nigerian eye clinics —
  prevalence studies show rates of 10–30% in rural and agricultural populations.
- The combination of intense UV radiation, harmattan dust, and predominantly
  outdoor occupations in Nigeria makes this a very high-prevalence condition.
- Surgical excision is the only cure — advise sunglasses and lubrication for
  mild cases; refer for surgery when vision is threatened or for cosmesis.
- Recurrence after surgery is common (especially with simple excision) — conjunctival
  autograft is the preferred surgical technique to reduce recurrence.
- Prevention education: wearing UV-protective wraparound sunglasses and wide-brimmed
  hats is effective and should be routinely promoted.
- Patients frequently present late when pterygium has already encroached on the
  pupil — early screening and education is important.
