# Trachoma

## Overview
Trachoma is a chronic infectious eye disease caused by the bacterium
Chlamydia trachomatis. It is the leading infectious cause of blindness worldwide
and remains a significant public health problem in parts of northern Nigeria,
particularly in rural, arid, and low-sanitation communities. It spreads through
contact with discharge from the eyes or nose of an infected person, flies,
shared towels, and contaminated water. Repeated infections over years lead to
scarring of the conjunctiva and cornea, eventually causing irreversible blindness.
Early active infections are treatable; late-stage disease requires surgery.

## Stages (WHO FISTO grading)
- TF (Trachomatous Inflammation – Follicular): Early active stage. Five or more
  follicles on the upper tarsal conjunctiva. Main stage in children. Treatable.
- TI (Trachomatous Inflammation – Intense): Pronounced inflammation, thickened
  tarsal conjunctiva. Active, highly infectious stage.
- TS (Trachomatous Scarring): White linear or stellate conjunctival scars.
  Result of previous infections. Not infectious but indicates exposure.
- TT (Trachomatous Trichiasis): Eyelashes touching the eyeball due to scarring
  causing lid inversion (entropion). Causes painful corneal abrasion. Needs surgery.
- CO (Corneal Opacity): Corneal scarring causing visual impairment or blindness.
  End-stage, irreversible.

## Symptoms to watch for
- Active stage (TF/TI): Red eyes, discharge, swollen eyelids, follicles inside
  upper eyelid, photophobia, foreign body sensation
- Often starts in childhood — may be asymptomatic or dismissed
- Trichiasis (TT): Lashes scratching the eye, extreme pain, tearing, photophobia,
  progressive corneal opacification
- Late corneal opacity (CO): Gradual vision loss, white/grey corneal opacity

## Triage guidance
- Trichiasis (eyelashes touching cornea) → URGENT, surgical referral for lid rotation
  — every day of delay worsens corneal scarring
- Active trachoma in child with follicles and discharge → URGENT, antibiotic treatment
  and household screening
- Community reporting of multiple cases in same household/village → URGENT,
  public health notification needed
- Corneal opacity from trachoma, established → ROUTINE, visual rehabilitation
  and assessment for keratoplasty (corneal transplant)
- Screening in at-risk community (northern Nigeria, rural, poor sanitation) → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Living in areas with poor water supply, sanitation, and hygiene (WASH)
- Children aged 1–9 years (most commonly infected age group; reservoir of infection)
- Sharing sleeping spaces with infected individuals
- Fly exposure around the face (flies transmit discharge)
- Living in dry, arid conditions (northern Nigeria especially)
- Female sex (women more affected in adults due to closer contact with infected children)
- Household crowding
- Absence of latrines or open defecation near living areas
- Low socioeconomic status

## What the AI should ask
- Where are you from — specifically, which state and is it rural or urban?
  (trachoma is most prevalent in northern Nigeria — Jigawa, Sokoto, Kebbi, Zamfara, Katsina, Kano)
- Do your eyelashes rub against your eyeball?
- Do you have pain from eyelashes scratching the eye?
- Do you have redness and discharge in the eye?
- How old is the patient? (most active disease in children)
- Have other family members had similar red eye problems?
- What is access to water like where you live?
- Has anyone in your household or community been treated for trachoma?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Bacterial conjunctivitis (acute, purulent — trachoma is chronic with follicles)
- Viral conjunctivitis (follicular but resolves; trachoma is persistent)
- Vernal keratoconjunctivitis (allergic; intense itching; young males; different follicle location)
- Entropion from other causes (elderly, post-injury — not infection-related)
- Corneal scar from other causes (ulcer, trauma, onchocerciasis)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Nigeria is one of the countries with the highest burden of trachoma globally.
  The north (especially NW and NE zones) is most affected.
- The WHO SAFE strategy guides trachoma control: Surgery, Antibiotics (azithromycin),
  Facial cleanliness, Environmental improvement.
- Azithromycin (single oral dose) is the first-line treatment for active trachoma —
  available through mass drug administration (MDA) campaigns in endemic areas.
- Trichiasis surgery (bilamellar tarsal rotation) is simple, effective, and can
  be performed by trained nurses in field settings.
- Sensitise patients to the fact that trichiasis means surgery is urgently needed,
  not just eye drops.
- Any clinic seeing patients from the Nigerian north should have a low threshold
  for trachoma investigation — it is frequently missed in urban southern clinics
  treating patients who have relocated.
