# Refractive Errors

## Overview
Refractive errors are the most common eye conditions globally and the most
frequent reason patients visit an eye clinic. They occur when the shape of the
eye prevents light from focusing correctly on the retina, resulting in blurred
vision. They are fully correctable with spectacles, contact lenses, or refractive
surgery. Uncorrected refractive errors are the leading cause of visual impairment
worldwide — including in Nigeria — and are the most preventable cause of vision
disability. Most patients presenting with blurred vision will have a refractive
error, and identifying it early is critical.

## Types
- Myopia (short-sightedness / nearsightedness): Can see near clearly; distant
  objects are blurry. The eyeball is too long or the cornea too curved.
  Very common in school-age children and young adults.
- Hyperopia (long-sightedness / farsightedness): Distant objects may be clear
  (especially when young) but near vision is blurred. Eyeball is too short.
  Common in children and can cause squint (strabismus) if uncorrected.
- Astigmatism: Uneven curvature of the cornea or lens causes blur at ALL distances
  — both near and far. Often coexists with myopia or hyperopia.
- Presbyopia: Age-related loss of near focusing ability (usually starts around age 40).
  Cannot read small print without glasses. Normal ageing process. Very common
  presenting complaint in Nigerian adults over 40.
- Anisometropia: Significant difference in refractive error between the two eyes;
  can cause amblyopia (lazy eye) in children if uncorrected.

## Symptoms to watch for
- Blurred vision at distance (myopia) or near (hyperopia, presbyopia)
- Squinting to see clearly
- Headaches, especially after reading or screen use (uncorrected hyperopia/astigmatism)
- Eye strain or eye fatigue
- Double vision (can indicate astigmatism or binocular vision problem)
- Difficulty reading small print, especially after age 40 (presbyopia)
- Holding reading material at arm's length to see
- Children: sitting close to the TV, difficulty on the school board, poor school performance
- Rubbing eyes frequently (especially in children)
- Squint (strabismus) in children — can be caused by uncorrected hyperopia

## Triage guidance
- Refractive error with no other symptoms is almost always ROUTINE
- Child with squint or suspected amblyopia → URGENT, within 1 week (amblyopia
  treatment is time-critical in children under 8)
- Sudden worsening of refractive error without explanation in an adult → URGENT
  (may indicate keratoconus, nuclear sclerotic cataract, or diabetic lens changes)
- Myopia progression in a child, especially rapid → URGENT, myopia control assessment
- Routine glasses or contact lens prescription update → ROUTINE
- Presbyopia assessment in patient over 40 → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Family history (myopia and hyperopia are strongly hereditary)
- Prolonged near work (reading, screens) — strongly associated with myopia progression
  in children and young adults
- Limited time spent outdoors (less outdoor time correlates with more myopia in children)
- Age (presbyopia is universal after age 40)
- Prematurity (premature infants at risk for high myopia)
- Diabetes (fluctuating blood sugar causes lens shape changes affecting refraction)
- Keratoconus (progressive corneal thinning causing high irregular astigmatism)

## What the AI should ask
- Is your vision blurry up close, at a distance, or both?
- When did you first notice the blurring?
- Do you currently wear glasses or contact lenses?
- How old are you? (presbyopia is expected from age 40)
- Do you get headaches after reading or screen use?
- Has your vision changed quickly or gradually?
- Do you have diabetes?
- Is it a child? Do they squint or sit close to the TV?
- Are you having difficulty reading small print in church, newspaper, or your phone?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Cataracts (also causes blur, but lens opacity is the cause — not correctable with glasses)
- Glaucoma (visual field loss alongside blur in advanced cases)
- Diabetic retinopathy (blur from retinal cause, not lens shape)
- Keratoconus (progressive irregular astigmatism — worsening despite new glasses)
- Dry eye (variable blur that fluctuates with blinking)
- Amblyopia (poor vision in one eye that does not fully correct with glasses)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Myopia is significantly increasing in Nigerian children, especially in urban
  areas and those in academically demanding schools — this is a growing public
  health issue.
- Presbyopia is extremely common among Nigerians aged 40–60 and is a major
  source of economic disability (inability to read, do fine work, use phone).
  Reading glasses are affordable and transformative.
- Many Nigerians access cheap, unqualified glasses from market vendors —
  emphasise the importance of proper refraction by a trained optometrist.
- Keratoconus appears to have high prevalence in Nigerians — be alert in young
  patients (teens–30s) with rapidly worsening or irregularly correctable blur.
- Contact lens services are growing in urban Nigeria — ensure proper hygiene
  education to prevent contact lens-related corneal ulcers.
