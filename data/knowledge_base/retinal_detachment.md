# Retinal Detachment

## Overview
Retinal detachment is a medical emergency in which the retina — the thin layer
of tissue at the back of the eye that senses light — pulls away from its normal
position and separates from the underlying supportive tissue. Without prompt
treatment, permanent vision loss in the affected eye is inevitable. Every hour
of delay increases the risk of irreversible damage, especially if the macula
(central retina) becomes detached. This is one of the most time-critical
conditions an eye clinic will encounter.

## Types
- Rhegmatogenous (most common): A tear or hole in the retina allows fluid to
  seep underneath and peel it away. Associated with high myopia, trauma, ageing.
- Tractional: Scar tissue on the retinal surface pulls the retina away.
  Most common in proliferative diabetic retinopathy.
- Exudative (serous): Fluid accumulates under the retina without a tear.
  Associated with inflammation, tumours, or severe hypertension.

## Symptoms to watch for — KNOW THESE EXACTLY
- Sudden appearance of many new floaters (dark spots, threads, or cobwebs)
- Flashes of light (photopsia), especially in peripheral vision — often in the dark
- A dark shadow, curtain, or veil appearing across part of the visual field
  — starts peripherally and advances toward the centre
- Sudden blurring of vision
- If the macula is involved: sudden, severe, central vision loss
- NO PAIN in most cases — the absence of pain does not mean it is not serious

## Triage guidance
- Any patient reporting curtain/shadow across vision → EMERGENCY, refer immediately
- Sudden onset floaters + flashes + any visual field defect → EMERGENCY
- Sudden floaters + flashes without field defect → URGENT, same day (may be
  posterior vitreous detachment — needs urgent exam to rule out retinal tear)
- Diabetic patient with new floaters and blurred vision → EMERGENCY (may be
  vitreous haemorrhage or tractional detachment)
- Known high myope with sudden new floaters → URGENT, within 24 hours

## Risk factors
- High myopia (nearsightedness greater than -6 dioptres — very high risk)
- Previous retinal detachment in either eye
- Family history of retinal detachment
- Advancing age (posterior vitreous detachment is a precursor)
- Previous eye surgery (cataract surgery increases risk)
- Eye trauma or injury
- Proliferative diabetic retinopathy
- Lattice degeneration (thinning of peripheral retina)
- Sickle cell retinopathy

## What the AI should ask
- Did you suddenly see many new floaters — like dots, threads, or a cobweb?
- Are you seeing flashes of light, especially in the dark?
- Is there a dark shadow, curtain, or veil blocking part of your vision?
- When exactly did this start — hours ago, today, yesterday?
- Is your central vision affected?
- Are you very short-sighted (high myopia)?
- Have you had any eye injury recently?
- Do you have diabetes?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD): Similar floaters and flashes but retina
  is still attached — still needs urgent exam to confirm no tear
- Vitreous haemorrhage: sudden floaters, blurred vision — often from PDR or
  retinal tear; may obscure detachment on examination
- Migraine with aura: transient visual disturbances that fully resolve
- Uveitis: can cause floaters but with pain and red eye
- Macular degeneration: central vision loss but gradual, not sudden

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- High myopia is becoming more prevalent, especially in urban and educated
  populations — a growing risk factor for retinal detachment in Nigeria.
- Sickle cell retinopathy (common in Nigeria given high sickle cell prevalence)
  can cause tractional retinal detachment — always ask about sickle cell status.
- Access to vitreoretinal surgery is limited in Nigeria — refer urgently to
  a tertiary centre with vitreoretinal surgical capability (Lagos, Ibadan, Abuja).
- Educate all myopic and post-cataract patients on warning signs so they seek
  care immediately rather than waiting.
- Time is retina: emphasise that this is a true eye emergency requiring immediate
  action, comparable to a heart attack for the eye.
