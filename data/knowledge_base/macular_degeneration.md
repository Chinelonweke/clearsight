# Age-Related Macular Degeneration (AMD)

## Overview
Age-related macular degeneration (AMD) is a progressive eye disease that
damages the macula — the small central area of the retina responsible for
sharp, straight-ahead vision used for reading, recognising faces, and driving.
AMD is the leading cause of vision loss in people over 50 in high-income
countries and is increasingly recognised in older Nigerians. It does not cause
total blindness (peripheral vision is preserved) but the loss of central vision
severely impacts quality of life and independence.

## Types
- Dry AMD (atrophic): Most common form (85–90%); drusen (yellow deposits)
  accumulate under the retina causing gradual thinning and atrophy.
  Progresses slowly over years. No curative treatment yet.
- Wet AMD (neovascular): Less common (10–15%) but accounts for most severe
  vision loss. Abnormal blood vessels grow under the retina and leak fluid or
  bleed, causing rapid, severe central vision loss. URGENT — treatable with
  anti-VEGF injections if caught early.

## Symptoms to watch for
- Gradual blurring or loss of central vision (dry AMD)
- Distortion of straight lines — lines appear wavy, bent, or broken (metamorphopsia)
  — KEY WARNING SIGN especially for wet AMD
- Dark, blurry, or blank spot in the centre of vision (scotoma)
- Difficulty reading, seeing faces, or doing fine detail work
- Colours appearing less bright or vivid
- Need for increasingly bright light to read
- Sudden, rapid worsening of central vision → wet AMD, URGENT

## Triage guidance
- Sudden distortion of straight lines or rapid central vision loss → URGENT,
  same-day or next-day referral (wet AMD is treatable — time matters)
- New dark central spot appearing rapidly → URGENT
- Gradually worsening central vision over months, no sudden change → ROUTINE
- Known dry AMD requesting review or new monitoring → ROUTINE
- Patient over 60 with no vision symptoms requesting AMD screening → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Age over 50 (risk increases significantly with each decade)
- Smoking (doubles the risk — the most modifiable risk factor)
- Family history of AMD
- Female sex
- Cardiovascular disease and hypertension
- Obesity
- High dietary fat intake, low fruit and vegetable consumption
- Prolonged sun/UV exposure
- Light iris colour (lower risk in darker-eyed populations — relevant for Nigerians)
- Previous cataract surgery

## What the AI should ask
- Are you over 50 years old?
- Is your central vision blurry or missing in one or both eyes?
- Do straight lines like door frames, tiles, or text appear wavy or distorted?
- Is this happening in one eye or both eyes?
- Did this come on suddenly or gradually over a long time?
- Do you smoke or have you smoked in the past?
- Do you have a family history of macular degeneration?
- Do you have cardiovascular disease or high blood pressure?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Diabetic macular oedema (central vision loss in diabetics — distinguish by history)
- Central serous chorioretinopathy (younger patients, central distortion, often self-limiting)
- Macular hole (central scotoma, usually in older women)
- Epiretinal membrane (wrinkling of macula, distortion)
- Myopic maculopathy (in highly myopic patients)
- Stargardt disease (hereditary, younger onset)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- AMD is underdiagnosed in Nigeria — patients often attribute central vision
  loss to "old age" and do not seek care early.
- Dark-pigmented individuals (including Nigerians) have some protective effect
  from AMD due to higher macular pigment and choroidal melanin, but AMD still occurs.
- Wet AMD treatment (anti-VEGF injections: ranibizumab, bevacizumab) is available
  in major Nigerian centres but is costly — refer urgently when wet AMD is suspected.
- Amsler grid self-monitoring is a free, teachable tool for patients with dry AMD
  to detect conversion to wet AMD early — teach all AMD patients to use it daily.
- Dietary advice: leafy greens (ugu, waterleaf), fish, and avoiding smoking are
  the most evidence-based lifestyle interventions.
