# Chemical Eye Injury (Chemical Burn / Ocular Chemical Trauma)

## Overview
Chemical injury to the eye is one of the most time-critical ocular emergencies.
Exposure to acid or alkali chemicals can rapidly and permanently destroy the
cornea, conjunctiva, and deeper structures, leading to severe vision loss or
blindness within minutes. The single most important intervention —
IMMEDIATE, COPIOUS IRRIGATION WITH WATER — must begin within seconds of exposure,
BEFORE the patient reaches a clinic. Every minute of delay worsens the outcome.
Alkali burns (lime, cement, cleaning agents, batteries) are far more dangerous
than acid burns because alkalis penetrate deeper and continue to damage tissue
long after initial contact.

## ⚠️ THIS IS ALWAYS AN OPHTHALMIC EMERGENCY ⚠️
Any patient reporting chemical in their eye must be treated as an emergency
regardless of current symptoms. Do not ask extended triage questions before
instructing immediate irrigation.

## Types of chemical injury
- Alkali burns (HIGH SEVERITY): Lime (calcium hydroxide — the most common in
  Nigeria due to construction and whitewashing), cement, drain cleaners (NaOH),
  ammonia, bleach, fertilizers, battery fluid (potassium hydroxide).
  Alkalis penetrate deeply, saponify cell membranes, and continue damaging
  tissue for hours. MOST DANGEROUS.
- Acid burns (MODERATE SEVERITY): Battery acid (H2SO4), vinegar (acetic acid),
  toilet cleaners (HCl). Acids cause immediate coagulation that forms a barrier,
  limiting deeper penetration. Still serious.
- Irritants (LOWER SEVERITY): Soap, detergent, hairspray, cosmetics, pool
  chemicals (chlorine). Less destructive but still need irrigation.

## Most common chemical causes in Nigeria
- Lime/calcium hydroxide (builders' lime, whitewashing) — most common and most severe
- Battery acid from motorcycle or car batteries
- Cement (also alkaline — similar to lime)
- Petrol / fuel splash (irritant/acid)
- Bleach (jik/chlorine-based cleaning products)
- Herbal eye preparations (can cause chemical irritation and corneal damage)

## Symptoms to watch for
- Immediate severe burning eye pain
- Intense tearing, blepharospasm (involuntary eye closure from pain)
- Extreme photophobia
- Redness, visible conjunctival swelling and chemosis
- Corneal clouding or whitening (sign of severe alkali burn — grave prognosis)
- Decreased vision (varies from mild blur to complete loss depending on severity)
- In severe burns: perilimbal whitening (ischaemia — very poor prognostic sign)
- Late complications (hours to days): corneal ulceration, perforation, symblepharon
  (lids sticking to eyeball), glaucoma, persistent vision loss

## Triage guidance — FIRST PRIORITY
- ANY chemical in the eye → EMERGENCY — IMMEDIATE MANAGEMENT:
  1. START IRRIGATION NOW — do not delay for history or examination
  2. Use any available clean water, saline, or Ringer's lactate
  3. Irrigate continuously for minimum 30 minutes (alkali) or 15 minutes (acid)
  4. Evert the eyelids and irrigate the conjunctival fornices (corners/pockets)
  5. Remove any particulate chemical (lime, cement) from the eye with wet swab
  6. After irrigation — assess pH with litmus paper (aim for pH 7.0–7.4)
  7. Re-irrigate until pH normalises
  8. REFER URGENTLY to ophthalmologist after initial irrigation

## Severity grading (Roper-Hall classification)
- Grade I: Clear cornea, no limbal ischaemia — good prognosis
- Grade II: Corneal haze, <1/3 limbal ischaemia — good prognosis
- Grade III: Corneal opacity (iris details obscured), 1/3–1/2 limbal ischaemia — guarded
- Grade IV: Opaque cornea (no iris visible), >1/2 limbal ischaemia — very poor prognosis

## Risk factors / circumstances
- Construction work (lime, cement — extremely common in Nigeria)
- Battery maintenance (auto mechanics, motorcyclists)
- Domestic cleaning and cooking (bleach, acids)
- Agricultural chemical use (fertilizers, pesticides)
- Children at play near construction sites or cleaning products
- Industrial workplaces without eye protection
- Assault (intentional chemical throwing — sadly occurs in conflict situations)

## What the AI should ask (AFTER irrigation has been initiated)
- What chemical entered the eye? (alkali vs acid changes urgency and management)
- When did the exposure happen — how many minutes or hours ago?
- Has the eye been irrigated with water yet? For how long?
- Is there pain, and how severe — can they open the eye?
- Can the patient see anything?
- Was it one eye or both eyes?
- Are there any chemical burns on the face or skin?
- Is the patient a child or adult?
- Was it accidental or intentional?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Thermal burn (heat, steam, welding flash — different mechanism, similar urgency)
- UV radiation keratopathy / photokeratitis (welder's flash, snow blindness — painful
  but delayed onset, typically 6–12 hours after exposure; no chemical involved)
- Corneal abrasion (mechanical, not chemical — less urgent)
- Acute angle-closure glaucoma (pain but no chemical exposure history)
- Infectious keratitis (pain and photophobia but no chemical exposure)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Lime/calcium hydroxide burns from construction work are the most common severe
  chemical eye injury in Nigeria. Nigeria's rapid construction growth makes this
  very prevalent. Builders and labourers frequently lack eye protection.
- Cement burns behave like lime burns — alkaline and deeply penetrating.
  Particulate cement in the eye must be physically removed after irrigation.
- Intentional acid/chemical attacks, while uncommon, do occur in Nigeria —
  be sensitive and ensure patient safety when this is suspected.
- Many patients arrive late after attempting to treat the injury with traditional
  methods or after waiting to see if it resolves — emphasise to all patients that
  immediate water irrigation is life-saving for the eye.
- Hospitals in many areas lack pH strips — irrigate based on chemical type and
  duration rather than waiting for pH confirmation.
- Free eye protection (goggles) distribution at construction sites and awareness
  campaigns for builders and auto mechanics would significantly reduce incidence.

## Post-irrigation management principles (for clinic awareness)
- Topical antibiotics to prevent secondary infection
- Cycloplegic drops for pain relief (ciliary spasm)
- Steroids (cautiously, first 10 days) to reduce inflammation
- Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) — promotes corneal healing
- Intensive lubrication
- Bandage contact lens in selected cases
- Surgical interventions for severe cases: amniotic membrane transplantation,
  limbal stem cell transplantation (for severe limbal ischaemia)
