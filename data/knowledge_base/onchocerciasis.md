# Onchocerciasis (River Blindness)

## Overview
Onchocerciasis, commonly known as river blindness, is a parasitic disease
caused by the filarial worm Onchocerca volvulus, transmitted by the bite of
blackflies (Simulium species) that breed in fast-flowing rivers. It is the
second leading infectious cause of blindness worldwide. Nigeria has the highest
burden of onchocerciasis in Africa, with over 7 million people infected,
primarily in rural communities near fast-flowing rivers across all geopolitical
zones. The ocular complications — punctate keratitis, sclerosing keratitis,
and optic atrophy — can lead to complete irreversible blindness over years
of repeated infection.

## Pathophysiology (simplified for AI context)
Blackfly bites deposit larvae under the skin. These mature into adult worms
that live in subcutaneous nodules (onchocercomata) for up to 15 years, producing
millions of microfilariae. When microfilariae die — either naturally or after
treatment — the resulting inflammatory reaction damages the eye tissues.
Ocular manifestations result from microfilariae invading the eye and the
inflammatory response they trigger.

## Ocular manifestations (symptoms to watch for)
- Punctate keratitis: Corneal snowflake-like opacities around dead microfilariae
  — reversible in early stages
- Sclerosing keratitis: Progressive, irreversible corneal scarring causing grey-white
  opacification starting at the limbus (sides) and advancing centrally
- Anterior uveitis: Irregular pupil, photophobia, redness — from microfilariae
  in the anterior chamber
- Chorioretinitis and optic atrophy: Retinal atrophy, pigmentary changes, optic
  nerve damage — leading to permanent vision loss and field defects
- Gradual, progressive loss of vision over years
- Severe itching (pruritus) — from microfilariae in the skin — is a dominant
  non-ocular symptom
- Skin changes: depigmentation ("leopard skin"), lichenification, hanging groin

## Systemic symptoms (important context)
- Intense skin itching — often the first and most prominent complaint
- Subcutaneous nodules (firm, non-tender lumps under skin — usually over bony prominences)
- Skin thickening, depigmentation, or "lizard skin" appearance
- In advanced cases: visual field loss, complete blindness

## Triage guidance
- Acute severe eye symptoms (uveitis, vision loss) in patient from endemic area → URGENT
- Progressive vision loss in patient from rural river-basin community → URGENT
- Known onchocerciasis patient with new visual symptoms → URGENT
- Patient from endemic area with skin itching and subcutaneous nodules, no eye
  symptoms yet → ROUTINE, screen and refer for ivermectin treatment
- Screening in at-risk patients from endemic areas → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Living near fast-flowing rivers in sub-Saharan Africa (rivers, streams, waterfalls)
- Farming, fishing, or outdoor work near rivers in endemic areas
- Most affected states in Nigeria: Plateau, Cross River, Benue, Taraba, Edo, Ondo,
  Kwara, Niger, Delta, Ebonyi, Enugu, Anambra, Abia, Imo (all zones affected)
- Age (cumulative exposure — young adults and middle-aged adults most severely affected)
- Male sex (more outdoor/riverine exposure)
- Not receiving ivermectin through community-directed treatment programmes (CDTI)

## What the AI should ask
- Where do you live — is it near a river, stream, or waterfall?
- Do you work near fast-flowing rivers or streams?
- Do you have severe itching of your skin?
- Do you have any lumps under your skin (especially over the hips, shoulders, or head)?
- Have you noticed any changes in your skin colour — pale patches?
- Has your vision been gradually getting worse over months or years?
- Have you received any ivermectin tablets through a community programme?
- Do you have any redness or pain in the eye, or sensitivity to light?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Trachoma (also causes corneal scarring but from bacterial infection, different pattern)
- Other causes of uveitis (toxoplasmosis, TB, syphilis — overlapping clinical features)
- Cataract (corneal vs lens opacity — distinct on examination)
- Glaucoma (optic atrophy — check IOP to differentiate)
- Vitamin A deficiency (corneal xerosis — nutritional, different pattern)
- Other filariases (loiasis — loa loa, mansonelliasis)

## Treatment context for triage
- Ivermectin (Mectizan) is the drug of choice — single annual or bi-annual oral dose
- Community-directed treatment with ivermectin (CDTI) is the mainstay of control
- Doxycycline (kills Wolbachia endosymbiont bacteria in the worm) — used in
  selected cases for more sustained effect
- Surgical nodulectomy for large, problematic nodules
- Ocular complications are treated symptomatically (steroids for uveitis,
  surgical rehabilitation for corneal scarring)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Nigeria has the highest number of onchocerciasis cases in Africa —
  this is not a rare tropical curiosity but a common clinical reality.
- The Onchocerciasis Control Programme and APOC have reduced transmission
  significantly, but endemic foci remain active.
- Many patients from rural areas may not spontaneously mention skin itching as
  an eye-related complaint — specifically ask about itching and skin changes.
- The visual loss from onchocerciasis is gradual and painless — patients often
  present very late when damage is advanced.
- Ivermectin availability through government CDTI programmes should be confirmed
  and patients enrolled where possible.
