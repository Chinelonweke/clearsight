# Diabetic Retinopathy

## Overview
Diabetic retinopathy (DR) is a diabetes complication that affects the blood
vessels of the light-sensitive tissue at the back of the eye (the retina).
It is the leading cause of new blindness in adults of working age globally
and is increasingly prevalent in Nigeria as the diabetes epidemic grows.
Early-stage DR has no symptoms — by the time patients notice vision changes,
significant damage may have already occurred. All diabetic patients need
regular eye screening even if they feel fine.

## Types / Stages
- Non-proliferative diabetic retinopathy (NPDR): Early stage — microaneurysms,
  haemorrhages, hard exudates. No new vessel growth yet. Mild, moderate, severe.
- Proliferative diabetic retinopathy (PDR): Advanced — new fragile blood vessels
  grow on the retina and vitreous. High risk of bleeding and retinal detachment.
- Diabetic macular oedema (DMO): Swelling of the macula; causes central vision
  loss and can occur at any DR stage. Most common cause of DR-related vision loss.

## Symptoms to watch for
- Early DR: often NO symptoms — this is critical to communicate to patients
- Floaters or dark spots (from vitreous haemorrhage)
- Blurred or fluctuating vision
- Difficulty seeing at night
- Colours appearing washed out
- Dark or empty area in the centre of vision (DMO)
- Sudden, severe vision loss — vitreous haemorrhage or tractional retinal detachment
  → EMERGENCY

## Triage guidance
- Sudden vision loss in a diabetic patient → EMERGENCY, same-day referral
- Vitreous haemorrhage (new floaters + blurred vision in diabetic) → EMERGENCY
- Known PDR or severe NPDR with new symptoms → URGENT, within 48 hours
- Diabetic patient with blurred vision not previously screened → URGENT, within a week
- Known diabetic with no eye symptoms, never screened → URGENT, screen within 2 weeks
- Known stable DR on treatment, routine follow-up → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Duration of diabetes (the longer, the higher the risk)
- Poor blood sugar control (high HbA1c)
- High blood pressure (hypertension accelerates DR)
- High cholesterol / dyslipidaemia
- Pregnancy (can rapidly worsen existing DR)
- Kidney disease (nephropathy often coexists with DR)
- Smoking
- Anaemia

## What the AI should ask
- Do you have diabetes? How long have you had it?
- Are you on medication for diabetes? Do you check your blood sugar?
- When did you last have an eye check-up?
- Have you noticed any floaters, flashes, or dark spots in your vision?
- Is your vision blurred, and has it been changing recently?
- Do you also have high blood pressure or kidney problems?
- Are you pregnant or recently pregnant?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Retinal vein occlusion (also causes haemorrhages; may mimic DR)
- Hypertensive retinopathy (associated with severe hypertension)
- Retinal detachment (can complicate PDR — emergency)
- Cataracts (common comorbidity in diabetics)
- Glaucoma (higher prevalence in diabetics)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Type 2 diabetes is rapidly increasing in Nigeria, especially in urban areas.
- Many patients are undiagnosed or poorly controlled due to drug cost and access.
- Patients may not associate diabetes with eye problems — education is essential.
- Ask about traditional remedies for diabetes, which may affect sugar control.
- Emphasise that regular screening every year is mandatory even with good vision.
- Sickle cell disease patients with diabetes face compounded retinal risk.
