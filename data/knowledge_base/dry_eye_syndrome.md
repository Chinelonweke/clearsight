# Dry Eye Syndrome (Keratoconjunctivitis Sicca)

## Overview
Dry eye syndrome (DES) is a chronic condition in which the eyes do not produce
enough tears, or the tears evaporate too quickly, leading to inadequate
lubrication of the ocular surface. It is one of the most common reasons for
eye clinic visits and is increasingly prevalent in Nigeria due to rising screen
time (phones, computers), air conditioning use, harmattan (dry, dusty) season,
and contact lens wear. While not usually sight-threatening, it significantly
impacts quality of life and can lead to corneal damage if severe and untreated.

## Types
- Aqueous-deficient dry eye: Insufficient tear production; associated with
  Sjögren's syndrome, ageing, medications, or lacrimal gland damage.
- Evaporative dry eye: Most common type; tears evaporate too fast due to
  meibomian gland dysfunction (MGD), where the oil layer of tears is deficient.
- Mixed (most patients have a combination of both)

## Symptoms to watch for
- Burning, stinging, or scratchy sensation in the eyes
- Watery eyes (paradoxical — reflex tearing compensates for dryness)
- Stringy mucus in or around the eyes
- Sensitivity to light (photophobia)
- Redness
- Sensation of having something in your eyes (foreign body sensation)
- Blurred vision, especially with prolonged reading or screen use
- Eye fatigue after short periods of reading or computer work
- Discomfort with contact lenses
- Worsening symptoms in air conditioning, wind, or dry/dusty environments (harmattan)

## Triage guidance
- Severe pain, vision loss, or corneal opacity → URGENT (may indicate corneal
  damage from severe untreated dry eye — not routine)
- Dry eye with inability to tolerate contact lenses, affecting work → URGENT
- Moderate symptoms affecting daily life with no vision loss → ROUTINE, within 2 weeks
- Mild burning/grittiness, seasonal or screen-related → ROUTINE
- Chronic dry eye patient requesting review of drops → ROUTINE

## Risk factors
- Extended screen time (phones, computers, TV) — reduced blink rate
- Air conditioning or heated indoor environments
- Harmattan season (very common seasonal exacerbation in Nigeria)
- Age over 50 (especially postmenopausal women — hormonal changes affect tears)
- Female sex (hormone-related)
- Contact lens wear
- Refractive surgery (LASIK reduces corneal sensation temporarily)
- Antihistamines, antidepressants, antihypertensives, oral contraceptives
- Autoimmune conditions (Sjögren's syndrome, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus)
- Thyroid disease (Graves' disease causes prominent eyes and poor lid closure)
- Vitamin A deficiency (significant in low-resource settings)
- Diabetes (reduces corneal nerve sensitivity)

## What the AI should ask
- Do your eyes feel dry, gritty, or burning?
- Do your eyes water a lot despite feeling dry?
- How much time do you spend on screens daily (phone, computer)?
- Do symptoms get worse in air conditioning, wind, or during harmattan?
- Do you wear contact lenses?
- Are you on any medication (antihistamines, antidepressants, blood pressure drugs)?
- Do you have any joint pain, dry mouth, or autoimmune conditions?
- Have you had any previous eye surgery?

## Differential diagnoses to consider
- Allergic conjunctivitis (intense itching is dominant; dry eye is more burning)
- Blepharitis (eyelid margin inflammation — often coexists with dry eye)
- Viral conjunctivitis (discharge, infectious context)
- Corneal abrasion or foreign body (acute onset, more severe pain)
- Sjögren's syndrome (systemic dry eye — needs systemic workup)
- Thyroid eye disease (prominent eyes, lid retraction)

## Clinical notes for Nigerian context
- Harmattan (November–March) is a major seasonal trigger — anticipate increased
  presentations during this period and advise preventive use of lubricating drops.
- Vitamin A deficiency-related dry eye exists in low-resource communities and
  can progress to xerophthalmia and blindness if untreated — ask about diet.
- Screen-related dry eye is increasing rapidly due to smartphone penetration.
- First-line treatment is preservative-free lubricating eye drops (artificial tears)
  — advise patients to use them consistently, not just when uncomfortable.
- Warm eyelid compresses twice daily significantly help evaporative dry eye
  (meibomian gland dysfunction) — this is free and teachable.
